The Science of Headphone Sound: Understanding Frequency Response and Consumer Audio Quality

Introduction

The headphone market has exploded in the last few years, with a wide variety of options and a large portion of consumers willing to spend extra money for a good pair. Features such as wireless connectivity and active noise cancelling have increased their integrity in society. However, many consumers seem to be unaware of what makes a headphone sound good. This article explores the science of frequency responses, what makes the ‚Äúideal‚Äù sound, and how the consumer headphone market deviates from established standards.

Understanding ‚ÄúNeutral‚Äù Frequency Response

Frequency response is determined by placing the headphone on a head and torso simulator (HATS) and measuring the amplitude of every frequency played. This is plotted on a graph and compared against targets such as Diffuse Field, which simulates the conditions of an ideal room to determine how sound travels within the room (Silver, 2024a) This allows for headphones to easily be compared to each other (Olive, 2022). The hearing range can be split into three main categories: Bass, Mid-range, and Treble (McCuaig, 2023; Olive, 2022). A neutral sound profile is one where all frequencies sound equally balanced, as it is intended to be heard by audio engineers (McCuaig, 2023). Due to how sound interacts with our anatomy, a neutral sound is highly individual. This is referred to as a head-related transfer function (HRTF); everyone has a different HRTF (Silver, 2024a).

Target Curves

Many tests have been conducted to determine what most consumers would consider to be the ‚Äúideal‚Äù sound. Averages of these tests have been plotted to determine a target frequency response, known as a target curve. Most research for over-ear headphones has been conducted for Harman by Dr. Sean Olive and is recognised as the ‚ÄúHarman target,‚Äù with many iterations since its first introduction in 2013 (Silver, 2024a).

Tests are primarily conducted using two methods: allowing test participants to try multiple headphones with different sound profiles or allowing them to alter the sound in an app and rating the sound, with a higher number being better (Olive et al., 2017).

Sound Signatures

Many consumers find neutral sound boring and prefer a coloured sound deviating from the audio engineer‚Äôs intentions. Common sound signatures are bass-heavy, V-shaped, and treble-heavy (McCuaig, 2023). A bass-heavy sound has significantly boosted bass, resulting in a thumpy, boomy sound at the expense of clarity if the boost extends into the mid-range, referred to as sounding ‚Äúwarm‚Äù (McCuaig, 2023). A V-shaped sound signature is one where both the bass and treble are boosted, resulting in a ‚ÄúV-shaped‚Äù graph. This results in an exciting sound where you still get extra thump and rumble without the qualities of treble being lost in the mix, at the expense of the mid-range almost disappearing (McCuaig, 2023). A treble-boosted sound enhances detail at the expense of sounding piercing and fatiguing, preferred by many as it reveals flaws in music and compensates for age-related hearing loss (McCuaig, 2023; Olive, 2022). This type of sound is known as being ‚Äúbright.‚Äù

Why Frequency Response Graphs Aren‚Äôt Flat

Almost all charts will have a bump in the treble region, referred to as ‚Äúear gain.‚Äù This represents the region of frequencies that are naturally amplified by the pinna, so that higher frequency sounds, such as speech, can be better understood (Headphones.com, 2022).

These headphones feature a V-shaped sound signature, with Bose having the most significant bass boost. On their own, these measurements don't reveal much about their sound compared to a neutral reference. Due to limitations, I will compare the Sony WH-1000XM5 to the Sennheiser HD 600, renowned for its neutral tuning and sub-bass roll-off, which closely follows the Harman 2018 linear target.

As seen in Figure 2 & 3, the XM5 has a notable bass boost while having a recessed mid-range but similar treble to the HD 600. This results in a sound that is boomy, bloated, and lacking in clarity. The recession in the mid-range is done to create a sense of space in the headphone; however, this comes at the expense of vocal clarity (Silver, 2024b).

Given this information, we can assume that the other headphones are simply different amounts of the same problem, and we can conclude that most consumer headphones are significantly bass boosted. However, this directly contradicts the research done by Harman and Dr Sean Olive to determine consumer preference.

The least bass boosted headphone in the table, the Airpods Max, still exceeds the target and the treble is significantly reduced.

Many companies prefer this tuning style as it sounds less fatiguing due to fewer perceivable details. Another reason is Dr. Dre's promotion of bass as the main headphone quality. Beats' success stems from cheap earphones lacking bass, leading consumers to associate bass with higher quality (Izundu, 2014).

Conclusion

There is a disconnect between scientific research and the actual tuning of consumer headphones. Research suggests that most listeners will prefer a neutral or slightly v shaped sound when given the choice; the market still seems to be dominated by bass boosted sound signatures. This results in more consumers associating excessive bass with audio quality and allows manufacturers to make clarity improvements every year by slightly reducing the boost ‚Äì making consumers waste money on new headphones.

As the headphone market continues to evolve, manufacturers may eventually lean towards more scientifically correct tunings. Until then, consumers seeking accurate sound will have to cope with audiophile/HiFi offerings.
